Radio Transmitter

How does a radio actually work? Through this project, I gained a deeper understanding of the subject. The goal was to build an AM radio transmitter using two main components: an audio transformer and a 1 MHz crystal oscillator.

Photos

The pictures above show the 2 components soldered and connected via wires on a perforated board. The first picture on the left shows the two components: on the left, the audio transformer, and on the right, the crystal oscillator. The second picture shows the soldered joints on the back of the perforated board.

Schematic

The audio transformer couples the phone's audio signal into the oscillator circuit, modulating the amplitude of the carrier — the core principle behind AM transmission. The crystal oscillator outputs a 1 MHz square wave, which serves as the carrier signal. Unlike a sine wave, a square wave contains harmonic energy at every integer multiple of the fundamental frequency, so the transmitter radiates not just at 1 MHz but at 2, 3, 4 MHz and beyond. This is visible in the evenly spaced spikes on the spectrum analyzer picture below. In an ideal square wave only odd harmonics (1, 3, 5 MHz...) carry significant power, but real switching transitions produce ringing, which introduces even harmonics as well. A low-pass filter or LC tank circuit tuned to 1 MHz would suppress these harmonics and concentrate power at the fundamental, improving both transmission efficiency and spectral cleanliness.
